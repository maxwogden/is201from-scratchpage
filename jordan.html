June 14, 1998 · Delta Center · Salt Lake City, Utah

The Push-Off.
Did He or Didn't He?...He Did

With 5.2 seconds left and the Bulls trailing by one, Michael Jordan made one of the most iconic — and controversial — plays in NBA history.

Game 6 · 1998 NBA Finals

The Play

It was the final game of Michael Jordan's dynasty run with the Chicago Bulls. The Utah Jazz, led by Karl Malone and John Stockton, had the lead and the ball — until Malone coughed it up to Jordan with 18.9 seconds left.

Jordan dribbled to the right wing, isolated on Jazz defender Byron Russell, then made his move. With one hand, he pushes off Russell's left arm, creating just enough separation to rise up and drain a 17-foot jumper. Bulls win. Sixth championship. All because the officials made a mistake.

Michael Jordan — 6× NBA Champion, 6× Finals MVP.

"I'm pushing off. I'll be honest — I did push off." — Steve Kerr, reflecting on the playstyle of the era

The Debate

Decades later, basketball fans, analysts, and the players themselves still argue about what happened in those final seconds. Was it a foul, or was it clean?

// The Arguments — Both Sides

  1. Arguments that it WAS a foul:
    • Jordan's left hand clearly makes contact with Russell's arm.
    • Russell's body visibly reacts to the push before Jordan's shot.
    • The play was not whistled, but NBA rules at the time prohibited this contact.
  2. Arguments that it was clean (or legal):
    • NBA defenders use hand-checking constantly — contact goes both ways.
    • Russell was known to grab and hold Jordan on previous possessions.
    • No referee on the floor or in replay called the foul in real time.
    • Jordan made this type of separation move throughout his career without penalty.
  3. What the players said:
    • Byron Russell: "He pushed off. No question about it."
    • Karl Malone: Still convinced the Jazz were robbed of a chip.
    • Jordan himself: Has never denied using his off-hand for separation.
    • Phil Jackson: Called it "Michael being Michael."
